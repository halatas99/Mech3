Three Types of Regression:

  • Linear Regression
  • Polynomial Regression
  • Multivariable Regression

General Idea (Works for all 3 types of regression!)¶

You have a variable $y$ that is dependent on $m$ variables $[x_1, x_2, ... ,x_m]$

You want to find out the relation between $y$ and $[x_1, x_2, ... ,x_m]$, i. e.

$$ y = \mathbf{\beta}\cdot{\mathbf{x}} = \beta_0\cdot1 + \beta_1x_1 + \beta_2x_2 + ... + \beta_mx_m$$

where $\mathbf{x} = [1, x_1, x_2, ... ,x_m]^T$,

and $\mathbf{\beta} = [\beta_0, \beta_1, \beta_2, ... ,\beta_m]$.

You want to find out $\beta~$! And that has to be found through the data!

You collect $n$ sets of data ($n$ measurements), and have a table that looks like this:

Data Set No.   y   x_1   x_2 ...   x_m
1 $y^{(1)}$ $x^{(1)}_1$ $x^{(1)}_2$ ... $x^{(1)}_m$
2 $y^{(2)}$ $x^{(2)}_1$ $x^{(2)}_2$ ... $x^{(2)}_m$
...
n $y^{(n)}$ $x^{(n)}_1$ $x^{(n)}_2$ ... $x^{(n)}_m$

So now you want to have:

$$\left[ {\begin{array}{*{20}{c}} {{y^{(1)}}}\\ {{y^{(2)}}}\\ {...}\\ {{y^{(n)}}} \end{array}} \right] \approx \left[ {\begin{array}{*{20}{c}} {{\beta _0} \cdot 1 + {\beta _1}{x^{(1)}}_1 + {\beta _2}{x^{(1)}}_2 + ... + {\beta _m}{x^{(1)}}_m}\\ {{\beta _0} \cdot 1 + {\beta _1}{x^{(2)}}_1 + {\beta _2}{x^{(2)}}_2 + ... + {\beta _m}{x^{(2)}}_m}\\ {...}\\ {{\beta _0} \cdot 1 + {\beta _1}{x^{(n)}}_1 + {\beta _2}{x^{(n)}}_2 + ... + {\beta _m}{x^{(n)}}_m} \end{array}} \right] $$

You can rewrite the above in a nice matrix form:

$$\left[ {\begin{array}{*{20}{c}} {{y^{(1)}}}\\ {{y^{(2)}}}\\ {...}\\ {{y^{(n)}}} \end{array}} \right] \approx \left[ {\begin{array}{*{20}{c}} {{1}}&{{x^{(1)}}_1}&{{x^{(1)}}_2}&{...}&{{x^{(1)}}_m}\\ {1}&{{x^{(2)}}_1}&{{x^{(2)}}_2}&{...}&{{x^{(2)}}_m}\\ {1}&{...}&{}&{}&{}\\ {1}&{{x^{(n)}}_1}&{{x^{(n)}}_2}&{...}&{{x^{(n)}}_m} \end{array}} \right] \cdot \left[ {\begin{array}{*{20}{c}} {{\beta _0}}\\ {{\beta _1}}\\ {{\beta _2}}\\ {...}\\ {{\beta _m}} \end{array}} \right] $$

Let's just call it

$$\mathbf{y} \approx \mathbf{G} \cdot \mathbf{\beta}$$

Well, you cannot solve it directly, so here comes the

Least-Squares Estimation¶

The lease-squares estimation gives you that the best fit occurs when

$$ \mathbf{G}^T\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{G}^T\mathbf{G} \cdot \mathbf{\beta} $$

Then you just solve this equation system to get $\beta$:

$$ \mathbf{\beta} = ( \mathbf{G}^T \mathbf{G} )^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{G}^T \mathbf{y} $$

1. Linear Regression (Single Regression)¶

We don't have a tutorial question for this, but you will likely see a question in your exams!

Now your variable $y$ only depends on $1$ variable $x_1$

You want to find out the relation between $y$ and $x_1$, i. e.

$$ y = \mathbf{\beta}\cdot{\mathbf{x}} = \beta_0\cdot1 + \beta_1x_1$$

where $\mathbf{x} = [1, x_1]$,

and $\mathbf{\beta} = [\beta_0, \beta_1]$.

So now you want to have:

$$\left[ {\begin{array}{*{20}{c}} {{y^{(1)}}}\\ {{y^{(2)}}}\\ {...}\\ {{y^{(n)}}} \end{array}} \right] \approx \left[ {\begin{array}{*{20}{c}} {{\beta _0} \cdot 1 + {\beta _1}{x^{(1)}}_1 }\\ {{\beta _0} \cdot 1 + {\beta _1}{x^{(2)}}_1 }\\ {...}\\ {{\beta _0} \cdot 1 + {\beta _1}{x^{(n)}}_1 } \end{array}} \right] $$

You can rewrite the above in a nice matrix form:

$$\left[ {\begin{array}{*{20}{c}} {{y^{(1)}}}\\ {{y^{(2)}}}\\ {...}\\ {{y^{(n)}}} \end{array}} \right] \approx \left[ {\begin{array}{*{20}{c}} {{1}}&{{x^{(1)}}_1}\\ {1}&{{x^{(2)}}_1}\\ {...}&{}\\ {1}&{{x^{(n)}}_1} \end{array}} \right] \cdot \left[ {\begin{array}{*{20}{c}} {{\beta _0}}\\ {{\beta _1}} \end{array}} \right] $$

Let's just call it

$$\mathbf{y} \approx \mathbf{G} \cdot \mathbf{\beta}$$

You got the $\mathbf{G}$ matrix for linear regression, and you know how to do the rest!

Examples¶

  • Temperature v.s. Number of icecreams sold
  • Stress v.s. Strain
In [43]:
import matplotlib as plt
import numpy as np
from pylab import *

x = np.linspace(0,1,20)
y = x + np.random.normal(0, 0.05, 20)

from scipy import stats
b1, b0, r_value, p_value, std_err = stats.linregress(x,y)
y_fit = b0 + b1 * x
print(f'y_fit = {round(b0,2)} + {round(b1,2)}*x')
print(f'R_Square = {round(r_value**2,4)}')

plt.scatter(x, y)
plt.plot(x, y_fit, c='r')
plt.xlabel('x')
plt.ylabel('y')
plt.show()

y_fit = -0.03 + 1.07*x
R_Square = 0.9882

Linear regression is super easy to do in MATLAB, basically just :

X = [ones(length(x),1) x];
b = X\y

Check the example in https://www.mathworks.com/help/matlab/data_analysis/linear-regression.html to learn more!

2. Polynomial Regression (Tutorial Question 1)¶

What if the variable $y$ only depends on one variable $x$, but the relationship is clearly not linear?

Let's look at the plot:

plt.scatter(x, amp)
plt.xlabel('Normalized frquency')
plt.ylabel('Amplitude/mm')
plt.show()

[-3.0, -1.0, 1.0, 3.0]

You want to fit in a parabola:

$$ y = \mathbf{\beta}\cdot{\mathbf{x}} = \beta_0\cdot1 + \beta_1x + \beta_2 x^2$$

Well doesn't that look familiar?

Just choose $\mathbf{x} = [1, x, x^2]$, and $\mathbf{\beta} = [\beta_0, \beta_1, \beta_2]$, and you have the familiar form again:

$$ y = \mathbf{\beta}\cdot{\mathbf{x}}$$

So now you just need to change your $\mathbf{G}$ matrix to:

$$\mathbf{G} = \left[ {\begin{array}{*{20}{c}} {{1}}&{{x^{(1)}}} &{{x^{(1)}}^2}\\ {1}&{{x^{(2)}}} &{{x^{(2)}}^2}\\ {...}&{} & {}\\ {1}&{{x^{(n)}}}&{{x^{(n)}}^2} \end{array}} \right] $$

You just need to solve for the $\beta$ coefficients through the least-squares estimations!

import statsmodels.api as sm

X = data[['MOEj', 'CLTa']]
Y = MOR

X = sm.add_constant(X) # adding a constant so that X = [1 MOEj CLTa]
 
model = sm.OLS(Y, X).fit()
print(model.summary())

xx1, xx2 = np.meshgrid(np.linspace(X.MOEj.min(), X.MOEj.max(), 100),
